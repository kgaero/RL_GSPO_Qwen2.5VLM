# Reference Analysis

Reference Analysis for "A Case Study of Staged Metric-Gated GRPO for Visual Numeric Reasoning"

Ordering note: this document follows the bibliography order generated by `paper/iclr2026_template/iclr2026_conference.bst` for `staged_metric_gated_grpo_camera_ready.tex`, which is alphabetical by author/year rather than citation order.

Scope: this document covers the 14 bibliography entries cited in the camera-ready paper. It excludes the two Hugging Face footnote URLs because they are operational resources, not formal bibliography entries.

## 1. Qwen2.5-VL

### Title

Qwen2.5-VL Technical Report

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13923

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- The Qwen2.5-VL report describes a vision-language model family with improved object localization, document parsing, chart and diagram understanding, and long-video reasoning.
- Architecturally, it introduces a native dynamic-resolution ViT, window attention, and absolute time encoding for long videos, enabling second-level temporal localization over videos that can extend to hours.
- The 72B flagship model is reported to match or approach state-of-the-art systems such as GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet on several document- and diagram-heavy tasks, while the family is released in three sizes including a 7B instruction-tuned model.
- For our use case, the paper establishes the capabilities and inductive biases of the pretrained base model family before any GRPO fine-tuning.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Experimental setup, Theoretical grounding
- This reference justifies the base model choice. Our paper uses `Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct` because the backbone already has strong priors for charts, tables, diagrams, and structured extraction, which are central to `MathVista`.
- The limitation is equally clear: the report is about pretraining and architecture, not RL adaptation for strict numeric-answer contracts. It does not address how to maintain parseability 1.0, control truncation, or choose between non-monotonic checkpoints during GRPO continuation.
- Our work therefore builds on Qwen2.5-VL as a capable starting point, then studies what additional control logic is required to turn that pretrained competence into stable visual numeric reasoning under a low-memory LoRA regime.

## 2. ReVisual-R1

### Title

Advancing Multimodal Reasoning: From Optimized Cold Start to Staged Reinforcement Learning

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04207

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- ReVisual-R1 analyzes multimodal reasoning pipelines and identifies three concrete effects: strong cold-start initialization matters, standard multimodal GRPO can suffer from gradient stagnation, and post-multimodal text-only RL can still improve multimodal reasoning.
- The paper argues that carefully selected text data can outperform several multimodal reasoning models even before multimodal RL begins.
- It proposes a staged training pipeline that combines optimized cold start, multimodal RL, and subsequent text-only RL, then reports strong 7B results on `MathVerse`, `MathVision`, `WeMath`, `LogicVista`, `DynaMath`, and `AIME 2024/2025`.
- The work is notable for treating multimodal RL as one phase inside a broader reasoning-development pipeline rather than as the full training story.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Baselines or comparisons
- Conceptually, this is one of the closest references to our paper because it also treats reasoning improvement as a staged process rather than a single fixed objective.
- The difference is where the complexity lives. ReVisual-R1 adds optimized cold start and post-hoc text RL to overcome GRPO stagnation, whereas our paper keeps the pipeline narrower and makes checkpoint-side metrics the control signal for phase progression and reward escalation.
- A limitation relative to our contribution is auditability: the paper motivates staging at the pipeline level but does not focus on explicit continuation semantics such as `best_structure`, `best_correctness`, and `best_composite` checkpoint aliases. Our work centers exactly on those low-level decisions because they determine run-to-run stability in a constrained environment.

## 3. RuCL

### Title

RuCL: Stratified Rubric-Based Curriculum Learning for Multimodal Large Language Model Reasoning

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.21628

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- RuCL starts from the observation that RLVR with outcome-only supervision can invite reward hacking in multimodal reasoning.
- It proposes generalized rubrics that are stratified by the model's competence level and then dynamically reweighted during training, shifting emphasis from foundational perception to higher-level logical reasoning.
- The paper explicitly reframes curriculum learning as reward design rather than only data ordering.
- On visual reasoning benchmarks, RuCL reports a `+7.83%` average improvement over `Qwen2.5-VL-7B` and reaches `60.06%` accuracy.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Theoretical grounding, Baselines or comparisons
- RuCL is the closest cited work to our reward-controller idea because it treats curriculum as a function of reward emphasis instead of only dataset sequencing.
- It supports the core intuition behind our method: the reward components that matter early are not the same as those that matter once the model already produces structurally valid outputs.
- The difference is that RuCL uses rubric stratification and competence-driven reweighting, whereas our controller is deliberately simpler and fully auditable, operating over six concrete reward components: `format`, `parseable`, `finished`, `correctness`, `brevity`, and `tolerance`.
- A limitation relative to our paper is operational transparency. Our work ties reward changes to explicit thresholds such as `parseable >= 0.92` and `truncation <= 0.08` over a two-checkpoint window, which makes continuation decisions inspectable in saved artifacts.

## 4. Vision-R1

### Title

Vision-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in Multimodal Large Language Models

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.06749

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- Vision-R1 studies how to induce DeepSeek-R1-style reasoning behavior in MLLMs and argues that pure RL is insufficient without a strong multimodal cold start.
- The paper constructs `Vision-R1-cold`, a `200K` multimodal chain-of-thought dataset created through modality bridging and filtering, then applies GRPO on a `10K` multimodal math dataset.
- To control excessive reasoning length after cold start, it introduces Progressive Thinking Suppression Training (PTST) together with a hard formatting reward.
- The reported outcome is an average improvement of about `6%` across multimodal math reasoning benchmarks.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Baselines or comparisons
- Vision-R1 is one of the closest multimodal RL precedents for our paper because it combines cold-start supervision, GRPO, and explicit format-sensitive rewards.
- It supports our methodological claim that multimodal reasoning benefits from structure-aware training signals, but it solves the problem mainly through large-scale cold-start data construction and PTST.
- Our paper differs by assuming a fixed pretrained base model (`Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct`) and focusing on an auditable control layer: stage filtering, metric-triggered reward reweighting, and checkpoint aliases.
- A limitation relative to our work is reproducibility under constrained compute: Vision-R1 depends on a sizable `200K` cold-start dataset, while our pipeline is designed for a Kaggle T4 profile with `LoRA rank 8`, `image size 336`, and tiny checkpoint-side evaluation budgets.

## 5. Infi-MMR

### Title

Infi-MMR: Curriculum-based Unlocking Multimodal Reasoning via Phased Reinforcement Learning in Multimodal Small Language Models

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.23091

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- Infi-MMR targets multimodal small language models and argues that direct multimodal RL is too brittle without a staged curriculum.
- It uses three phases: Foundational Reasoning Activation on high-quality text reasoning data, Cross-Modal Reasoning Adaptation on caption-augmented multimodal data, and Multimodal Reasoning Enhancement on curated caption-free data.
- The resulting `Infi-MMR-3B` model reports `43.68%` on `MathVerse testmini`, `27.04%` on `MathVision test`, `21.33%` on `OlympiadBench`, and `67.2%` on `MathVista testmini`.
- The key insight is that multimodal reasoning benefits when textual reasoning competence is activated before the model is asked to rely fully on visual grounding.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Baselines or comparisons, Theoretical grounding
- Infi-MMR strongly supports our use of phased training: it is direct evidence that multimodal reasoning improves under non-stationary curricula rather than a single RL regime.
- Our paper differs in the unit of adaptation. Infi-MMR fixes a three-phase conceptual schedule with different data sources, while we adapt reward weights checkpoint by checkpoint using measured parseability, tag compliance, malformed rate, and exact-match stagnation.
- A limitation relative to our contribution is control granularity. Infi-MMR motivates staging at a high level, but it does not expose the kind of explicit, auditable controller rules that our paper logs and persists for every evaluated checkpoint.

## 6. MathVista

### Title

MathVista: Evaluating Mathematical Reasoning of Foundation Models in Visual Contexts

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.02255

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- MathVista defines a benchmark for mathematical reasoning in visual contexts and combines tasks involving diagrams, charts, tables, figures, and other visual artifacts.
- The benchmark contains `6,141` examples drawn from `28` existing multimodal datasets plus `3` newly created datasets: `IQTest`, `FunctionQA`, and `PaperQA`.
- The paper evaluates `12` foundation models and reports that `GPT-4V` achieves `49.9%` overall accuracy, still `10.4%` below human performance.
- Its key empirical observation is that strong language models remain brittle when fine-grained perception and compositional reasoning must be combined.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Problem formulation, Experimental setup
- MathVista is the direct benchmark foundation for our work. Our curriculum stages are filtered views over MathVista, and our problem is framed explicitly as visual numeric reasoning over MathVista-style question types and answer modes.
- The benchmark also motivates our structure-first design: because many items require both visual extraction and numeric reasoning, malformed or truncated outputs destroy evaluability before correctness can even be measured.
- A limitation of MathVista for our purposes is that it is an evaluation benchmark, not a training method. Our paper supplies the missing training-system layer: stage construction, response contracts, reward decomposition, checkpoint evaluation, and controller logic.

## 7. Asynchronous RLHF

### Title

Asynchronous RLHF: Faster and More Efficient Off-Policy RL for Language Models

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.18252

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- This paper studies online but off-policy RLHF by decoupling sample generation from policy learning, allowing asynchronous generation and training to proceed in parallel.
- The authors analyze how much off-policyness can be tolerated before the learning signal degrades and find that online DPO is especially robust in that regime.
- The study emphasizes compute scaling rather than only reward quality, arguing that asynchronous RLHF can improve throughput and training efficiency.
- It also shows a trade-off: extra compute optimizations speed training, but some of them reduce final alignment performance.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Theoretical grounding, Baselines or comparisons
- Our paper cites Asynchronous RLHF as part of the broader RLHF landscape to show that recent work has focused on optimization efficiency, off-policy robustness, and new learning regimes.
- The contrast is important: our case study does not target asynchronous or off-policy training. We use checkpoint-wise synchronous evaluation because the dominant problem in our setting is not throughput but instability in parseability, formatting, and completion.
- A limitation for our use case is that Asynchronous RLHF does not model multimodal structural validity metrics such as malformed output rate or solution-tag compliance. Our controller explicitly gates reward changes on those metrics before it increases correctness pressure.

## 8. CogCoM

### Title

CogCoM: A Visual Language Model with Chain-of-Manipulations Reasoning

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.04236

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- CogCoM argues that conventional vision-language alignment encourages conclusive answers without faithful visual reasoning, especially on detailed visual problems.
- It introduces Chain of Manipulations, where the model learns intrinsic operations such as grounding and zooming, along with the associated intermediate evidence, without relying on external tools.
- The paper combines a multi-turn multi-image architecture, an automated data-generation pipeline, and `6K` manually annotated samples for challenging graphical math tasks.
- The resulting `17B` model achieves strong performance across `9` benchmarks from `4` categories while preserving interpretability through visible intermediate manipulations.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Baselines or comparisons, Methodology
- CogCoM is cited as an explicit visual-reasoning alternative to curriculum-based RL. It addresses multimodal math failures by changing the model's reasoning interface and internal supervision target, not by changing checkpoint-level control logic.
- Our paper makes the opposite trade-off: we keep the base VLM architecture standard and focus on training dynamics around output validity, parseability, and continuation.
- A limitation relative to our setting is model and system complexity. CogCoM's `17B` architecture and manipulation mechanism are significantly heavier than our `Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct + LoRA` setup, while our work cannot claim the same step-level interpretability of visual operations.

## 9. DeepSeekMath

### Title

DeepSeekMath: Pushing the Limits of Mathematical Reasoning in Open Language Models

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.03300

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- DeepSeekMath introduces `DeepSeekMath 7B`, built by continuing pretraining from `DeepSeek-Coder-Base-v1.5 7B` on `120B` math-related tokens mined from Common Crawl plus natural-language and code data.
- The paper reports `51.7%` accuracy on the competition-level `MATH` benchmark without external tools or voting, rising to `60.9%` with self-consistency over `64` samples.
- Its main algorithmic contribution is Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), a PPO-style RL method that compares completions within a sampled group rather than against a separate value baseline.
- The work is centered on text mathematical reasoning, not vision-language reasoning, but it establishes GRPO as a practical optimizer for verifiable reasoning tasks.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Theoretical grounding
- This is the direct source of the GRPO optimizer used by our fine-tuning pipeline; our paper explicitly keeps the GRPO objective unchanged and studies the surrounding system design instead.
- DeepSeekMath supports the claim that relative-reward RL can improve reasoning, which underwrites our use of `TRL`'s `GRPOTrainer` for numeric-answer optimization.
- Its limitation for our setting is that it does not address multimodal failure modes such as malformed XML-like tags, parse failures, or truncation. Our paper adds metric gates for `parseable`, `format`, and `finished` rewards precisely because those structural issues dominate early multimodal RL.
- Our work differs in scope: DeepSeekMath changes the optimizer for text math, while our paper studies staged curriculum control, reward reweighting, and alias-based checkpoint continuation on MathVista.

## 10. SATORI-R1

### Title

SATORI-R1: Incentivizing Multimodal Reasoning through Explicit Visual Anchoring

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.19094

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- SATORI-R1 argues that directly applying R1-style free-form RL to visual question answering is mismatched to multimodal tasks because long reasoning chains can diffuse visual focus and because unverifiable intermediate steps increase policy-gradient variance.
- To address this, it decomposes VQA into three verifiable stages: global image captioning, region localization, and answer prediction, each with an explicit reward signal.
- The paper also introduces `VQA-Verify`, a `12K` dataset annotated with answer-aligned captions and bounding boxes to support this stage-wise RL setup.
- Experiments report consistent gains across `7` VQA benchmarks, with up to `15.7%` accuracy improvement over an R1-like baseline, and attention-map analysis shows stronger focus on task-critical regions.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Theoretical grounding, Baselines or comparisons
- This is the strongest external support for the introduction claim that correctness reward is not immediately informative unless intermediate outputs are already structurally verifiable. SATORI-R1 makes that point directly by contrasting unverifiable free-form chains with explicit staged rewards.
- It also supports our claim that unstable structural preconditions make RL updates harder to interpret, since the paper explicitly links unverifiable intermediate steps to amplified policy-gradient variance.
- The main difference is architectural granularity. SATORI-R1 decomposes VQA into captioning, localization, and answer prediction with explicit visual anchoring, whereas our pipeline keeps a simpler free-form numeric-answer format and stabilizes it through checkpoint-gated reward control, structure floors, and alias-based continuation.
- A limitation relative to our setting is that SATORI-R1 changes the reasoning interface and supervision target more substantially than our method. Our contribution stays closer to a standard GRPO fine-tuning loop and studies how far auditable pipeline control can go without redesigning the task into three separately rewarded stages.

## 11. RL with Cold Start

### Title

Advancing Multimodal Reasoning via Reinforcement Learning with Cold Start

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.22334

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- This paper argues that multimodal "aha moment" self-correction patterns can appear before RL and are not, by themselves, evidence of better reasoning.
- Its core recipe is a two-stage pipeline: structured chain-of-thought SFT as a cold start, followed by GRPO to refine multimodal reasoning behavior.
- The paper reports strong gains at both `3B` and `7B` scales, including `MathVista` improvement from `66.3%` to `73.4%` and `We-Math` improvement from `62.9%` to `70.4%` for the `7B` model.
- The contribution is practical rather than theoretical: it shows that RL-only training is inferior to `SFT + RL` for multimodal reasoning.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Baselines or comparisons
- Our paper cites this work as evidence that multimodal RL is most effective when embedded in a staged pipeline rather than treated as a single monolithic training phase.
- The key contrast is that its stages are dominated by a large cold-start SFT component, while our stages are lightweight curriculum mixtures over filtered MathVista subsets plus a rule-based reward controller.
- A limitation for our paper's goals is that the reference does not analyze checkpoint non-monotonicity, parseability floors, or reward adaptation driven by explicit metrics such as `p < 0.85` or `t > 0.15`. Our paper addresses those engineering questions directly because they determine whether correctness reward is even informative.

## 12. SPPO

### Title

Self-Play Preference Optimization for Language Model Alignment

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00675

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- SPPO reframes alignment as a constant-sum two-player game and seeks a Nash equilibrium policy instead of fitting a standard Bradley-Terry preference model.
- The method performs iterative self-play updates and introduces an SPPO objective motivated by game-theoretic analysis of preference optimization.
- In experiments, SPPO uses only `60K` prompts from `UltraFeedback` together with the `PairRM` preference model (`0.4B` parameters) to fine-tune `Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2`.
- The resulting model reaches a `28.53%` length-controlled win rate against `GPT-4-Turbo` on `AlpacaEval 2.0` and outperforms iterative DPO and IPO on `MT-Bench`, `Arena-Hard`, and the Open LLM Leaderboard.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Theoretical grounding, Baselines or comparisons
- Our paper cites SPPO to position itself against recent alignment work that proposes new objectives. This matters because we are making the opposite design choice: we do not introduce a new RL objective and instead analyze training-stack controls around an existing one.
- SPPO is based on pairwise preference modeling for open-ended text responses, whereas our rewards are verifiable and sample-local: exact match, tolerance, parseability, tag compliance, and truncation on numeric visual QA.
- A limitation relative to our work is that SPPO does not address multimodal evaluation contracts or checkpoint selection in non-monotonic RL runs. Our contribution begins where SPPO stops: once the optimizer is fixed, how should one stage data, preserve structure rewards, and choose continuation checkpoints?

## 13. VL-Cogito

### Title

VL-Cogito: Progressive Curriculum Reinforcement Learning for Advanced Multimodal Reasoning

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.22607

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- VL-Cogito proposes `PCuRL` (Progressive Curriculum Reinforcement Learning) for multimodal reasoning tasks with heterogeneous difficulty and semantics.
- The paper explicitly motivates the method by arguing that multimodal tasks have inherent complexity and diversity, and that existing models therefore show unstable performance across domains and difficulty levels.
- The framework introduces two specific control mechanisms: online difficulty soft weighting across RL stages and a dynamic length reward that adjusts reasoning length to task complexity.
- The paper reports that the method matches or surpasses prior multimodal reasoning models on benchmarks spanning mathematics, science, logic, and general understanding.
- Its central claim is that curriculum difficulty and reasoning-length control should be co-optimized during RL rather than fixed beforehand.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Methodology, Baselines or comparisons
- This is one of the most directly comparable references because it also combines RL with progressive curriculum design and explicit length-sensitive rewards.
- It is also the cleanest support in our introduction for the claim that multimodal optimization is intrinsically more complex than unimodal text RL, because the abstract explicitly attributes instability to domain and difficulty diversity.
- The main difference is control style. VL-Cogito uses online soft weighting and adaptive length regulation, whereas our controller uses hard metric thresholds, reward floors, and two-checkpoint plateau detection before increasing correctness weight by `0.50`.
- A limitation for our specific goals is that the reference does not foreground output-structure diagnostics such as parseability, malformed completions, or checkpoint aliasing. Our paper emphasizes those metrics because structure failures are the main blocker in low-budget multimodal GRPO runs.

## 14. MAVIS

### Title

MAVIS: Mathematical Visual Instruction Tuning with an Automatic Data Engine

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.08739

### Summary (Concise but Informative)

- MAVIS proposes an automatic data engine for mathematical visual reasoning and avoids both human annotation and GPT API calls when building training data.
- The pipeline creates `MAVIS-Caption` with `558K` diagram-caption pairs and `MAVIS-Instruct` with `834K` visual math problems paired with chain-of-thought rationales.
- Training proceeds in four stages: contrastive tuning of a `CLIP-Math` vision encoder, vision-language alignment through a projection layer, instruction tuning, and finally DPO to refine chain-of-thought quality.
- The paper therefore tackles multimodal math reasoning primarily through synthetic data scale and progressive instruction tuning, not through RL checkpoint control.

### Relevance to Our Paper (Critical Analysis)

- Contributes to: Baselines or comparisons, Methodology
- MAVIS is cited to represent a different solution family for the same problem domain: instead of adapting the RL control loop, it expands task coverage through a large automatic data engine and a multi-stage supervised/DPO training stack.
- That contrast sharpens our paper's contribution. We do not build a new vision encoder, introduce a new dataset at `558K` or `834K` scale, or run DPO; we use a standard pretrained VLM and ask how far curriculum filters and metric-gated GRPO can go under tight resource limits.
- A limitation relative to our case study is deployment cost and reproducibility in small environments. Our Kaggle T4 profile is deliberately narrower and therefore addresses a more constrained but practically common setting than MAVIS.
